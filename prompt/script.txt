The soft roll in your bathroom right now represents barely more than a century of use,
out of roughly 300,000 years of human existence.
For the overwhelming majority of that time, people managed perfectly well using leaves, moss, stones, sponges, cloth, corncobs, water, or simply their bare hands,
adapting cleverly and constantly to whatever their environment provided.
None of it was disgusting to the people who used it.
It was simply normal, exactly the way rolled paper feels normal to you now.
Disgust, it turns out, is far more cultural than biological.
What repulses one generation was simply Tuesday for another,
and what feels indispensable to you today will very likely look primitive, wasteful, or strange to whatever comes next.
Some hygiene researchers already argue that water-based cleaning—
the method still standard across much of Asia, the Middle East, and parts of Europe—
is objectively more effective and more sustainable than dry paper alone,
suggesting the story of this particular invention is far from finished.
So the next time you reach for that roll without thinking, without even glancing at it,
you might spare half a second of appreciation.
Somewhere behind that small, unremarkable object sits an enormous, sprawling history of human problem-solving,
dating back long before paper, long before plumbing, long before privacy itself was even considered a right worth protecting.
Your ancestors managed just fine without it,
but it’s probably fair to say you wouldn’t want to go back.
